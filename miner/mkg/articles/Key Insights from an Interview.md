**Who Owns AI Infrastructure? Key Insights from an Interview with Proof of Human Founder Bogdan**

In the rapidly evolving world of artificial intelligence, a critical question is emerging: **Who truly owns the infrastructure powering AI?** As demand for compute resources skyrockets, centralized control by a few tech giants raises concerns about innovation, access, and power distribution.

A recent interview published by Kaprika Press with Bogdan, Founder of **Proof of Human** (@poh_network), dives deep into these issues. The conversation explores the shift from centralized AI giants to decentralized, community-owned computing networks.

### From Trust Verification to Decentralized Compute

The project started with solving a fundamental trust problem on blockchain: verifying whether an address belongs to a real human, an organization, or an automated system. Analyzing vast blockchain data revealed the need for massive computing power.

> "While analyzing massive amounts of blockchain data, we encountered the need for large-scale computing. That led to a question: if thousands of users already possess computing resources, why not combine them into a single network? That was the turning point." — Bogdan

This realization evolved Proof of Human into a distributed computing network focused on AI, where participants contribute GPU power for useful work like verifying humans against spam, deepfakes, and bots.

### The Risks of Centralized AI Control

Bogdan highlights the systemic risks of AI infrastructure being dominated by a handful of corporations:

- They control the pace of innovation
- They set the cost of access
- They influence the direction of technological development

This concentration creates dependency and limits broader participation. Control over infrastructure historically translates to economic and societal influence—especially as AI becomes foundational to the digital economy.

### Decentralization: Beyond Marketing Slogans

Proof of Human differentiates itself through **ownership economics**. True decentralization means distributing ownership of computing resources themselves, not just using the term as a buzzword.

> "If key resources are controlled by a single party, the system remains centralized regardless of the narrative. Our goal is to distribute ownership of computing power itself." — Bogdan

Unlike OpenAI or Google, which build powerful centralized products, Proof of Human explores an alternative architecture where resources belong to network participants.

### Benefits for Everyday Users

Participants aren't just consumers—they become part of the infrastructure:

- Contribute computing resources (e.g., GPUs)
- Earn rewards
- Gain ownership in the AI compute layer

This model addresses the current mismatch where demand for compute grows faster than supply, making access expensive and constrained.

### Challenges and the Road Ahead

Building a reliable decentralized network isn't easy. Key hurdles include:

- Reliability and computational quality
- Security
- Scalability

However, Bogdan believes the long-term advantages—openness, flexibility, and resilience—outweigh the complexities.

**Looking forward:**
- In 5 years: Coexistence of corporate and distributed models.
- In 10 years: The defining competition shifts from "Which AI is better?" to "**Who owns the AI infrastructure?**"

### The Mission

> "To build an infrastructure where people do not merely use artificial intelligence, but own part of the computational foundation that powers it." — Bogdan

This vision aims for a more inclusive digital economy where access to compute isn't gated by corporate scale but enabled by community participation.

### Why This Matters Now

With AI infrastructure buildouts reaching trillions in potential investment globally, projects like Proof of Human offer a compelling counter-narrative to Big Tech dominance. By leveraging existing user hardware in a decentralized network, it democratizes access and aligns incentives around real human participation and useful AI work.
